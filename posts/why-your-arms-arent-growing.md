Here's a scene that plays out in gyms everywhere. You've just finished a full push day, bench press, incline press, overhead press, maybe some lateral raises. You're an hour and fifteen minutes in. Your chest and shoulders are pumped. Your triceps have been working as secondary movers through every pressing movement on the program.

Then you grab a pair of dumbbells for skull crushers or cable pushdowns. And you wonder why, after months of this, your arms still look the same.

It's not genetics. It's fatigue sequence.

## The Problem With Training Arms After Push or Pull

When you hit direct arm work at the end of a push session, your triceps have already been under load for close to an hour. They've worked through every horizontal and vertical pressing movement on the menu. By the time you get to isolation work, you're not training fresh triceps, you're training a muscle that has already been used as a support actor in three or four other exercises.

The same problem hits biceps at the end of a pull day. Rows, pulldowns, face pulls and your biceps have been flexing and stabilizing through all of it. Arriving at your curl set with that accumulated fatigue means you can't load the muscle with the tension it needs to actually adapt. The weight you can use is compromised. The quality of each rep is compromised. Your form compensates in small ways, a little shoulder swing, a shortened range of motion because the target muscle is already running low.

The result: the muscle receives a stimulus, but not the right kind. Volume without adequate mechanical tension doesn't build arms the way most lifters expect. You're getting some work in, but you're not training arms,  you're finishing them.

## What Old Iron Does Differently

Old Iron places direct arm work on squat day and deadlift day.

This is a deliberate structural choice, not an accident of scheduling. When you finish your squat sets, your biceps and triceps have done essentially nothing. Lower body compound movements don't fatigue the arms in any meaningful way. They're passengers on a leg day. They're completely fresh.

That freshness changes everything.

When you load a fully recovered bicep with a barbell curl or an incline dumbbell curl, you can achieve full mechanical tension through the entire range of motion with actual load behind it. You're not working around accumulated soreness. You're not compensating with shoulder involvement because the bicep can't handle the weight. You're training the muscle you're trying to build, in the state it needs to be in to respond.

Triceps get the same advantage on deadlift day. Overhead extensions, cable pushdowns, and close-grip work performed on a completely fresh upper body hit categorically differently than the same exercises performed as an afterthought after pressing. The difference isn't subtle once you feel it.

## The Volume and Frequency Math

Old Iron programs three sets of direct bicep work and three sets of direct tricep work per arm day. With two dedicated arm sessions per week, that's twelve weekly sets for biceps and twelve for triceps.

Research on hypertrophy volume consistently points to 10–20 working sets per muscle group per week for most intermediate lifters. Twelve sets sits squarely in the productive range enough to drive adaptation without crowding out recovery for the primary lifts that the whole program is built around.

But volume is only part of the equation. It's the quality of that volume that separates meaningful training from busy work. Twelve sets performed with a fresh muscle, with full range of motion, with proper loading and that's worth far more than twenty sets hammered out at the end of a long session when fatigue has already compromised everything.

Frequency matters too. Two dedicated arm sessions per week creates two separate protein synthesis spikes, which consistently produces better hypertrophic outcomes than one high-volume session crammed into a single day.

## The Honest Assessment

If your arms have stalled, ask yourself honestly: when in your session are you actually training them? After pressing? After pulling? At the 75-minute mark when you're already depleted?

The answer probably isn't more sets at the end of push day. It's different placement, proper loading, and a fresh starting point.

That's what Old Iron delivers and why the arm programming gets the results that most push/pull splits don't.
